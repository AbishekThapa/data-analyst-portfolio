Queensland Road Crash Analysis

A comprehensive analysis of road crash data (2001-2024) to identify key trends, risk factors, and geospatial hotspots.

1. Crashes by Day of Week

Findings: Crash counts peak significantly on Friday, followed by Thursday and Wednesday, suggesting a strong correlation with the typical work week.

2. Top Suburbs by Crash Count

Findings: The analysis identifies a clear list of the top 10 suburbs with the most crashes, likely major commercial or transport hubs.

3. Crashes by Month

Findings: The data shows specific months with higher crash frequencies, which can help in planning seasonal road safety campaigns.

4. Yearly Crash Trends

Findings: The line plot reveals the long-term trend, highlighting years of progress in road safety and years where incidents spiked.

5. Hourly and Daily Crash Hotspots

Findings: The heatmap confirms the hypothesis, showing a concentration during the afternoon hours (3-6 PM) on weekdays, with Friday afternoon being the absolute peak.

6. Daily Crash Patterns in Top Suburbs

Findings: Most high-incident suburbs follow the general trend of a Friday peak, but some show a steeper increase than others, indicating different weekly traffic rhythms.

7. Factors Influencing Fatal Casualties

Findings: The average number of fatalities per crash increases dramatically with the speed limit. "Darkness - Not Lighted" is by far the most dangerous lighting condition for fatalities.

8. Geospatial Mapping of Recent Crashes

Findings: The interactive Folium map clearly shows that crashes are heavily clustered along major roads and intersections, allowing for drill-down into specific hotspots.

9. Crashes by Atmospheric Condition

Findings: The vast majority of crashes occur in clear weather, likely because this is the most frequent driving condition, not because it is inherently safer.

10. Crashes by Lighting Condition

Findings: A large majority of crashes occur during Daylight, which provides a baseline for understanding the relative frequency of crashes in other conditions.

11. Overall Project Limitations

  • Reporting Bias: The data only includes crashes that were reported. Minor incidents may be underrepresented.
  • Data Accuracy: There may be errors in the recorded data. Missing values in key columns limit the scope of certain analyses.
  • Correlation vs. Causation: This analysis identifies correlations but does not prove causation.

12. Future Work

Based on these findings, future analysis could explore:

  • Crash Rate Analysis: Normalize crash counts by traffic volume or road length to find truly dangerous areas.
  • Severity Modeling: Build a predictive model to identify the factors most indicative of a severe or fatal crash.
  • Holiday Impact: Analyze the specific impact of public holidays on crash frequency and location.
  • Time Series Forecasting: Use historical data to forecast future crash counts.
